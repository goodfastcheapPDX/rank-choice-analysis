Vote Strength Index

▼
🧒 Explain Like I'm 5

Imagine you're asking kids to rank their favorite ice cream flavors. If a flavor gets picked as #1 by lots of kids, that's stronger than getting picked as #5 by the same number. Vote Strength measures how much voters really, really liked a candidate vs. just thinking they were "okay."

Other ways to think about it: Enthusiasm Score, Preference Intensity, How Much Voters Really Wanted This Person

Low (40%)
Mostly low rankings Medium (60%)
Mixed rankings High (80%+)
Mostly high rankings
🔢 How We Calculate It

Step 1: Give points for each ranking (1st place = 6 points, 2nd = 5 points, 3rd = 4 points, etc.)

Step 2: Add up all the points the candidate earned

Step 3: Divide by the maximum possible points (if everyone ranked them 1st)

Formula: (Total Points Earned) ÷ (Maximum Possible Points) = Vote Strength

📝 Examples

HIGH Strength (91%): Gets 1000 first-choice, 500 second-choice, 200 third-choice
• Points: (1000×6) + (500×5) + (200×4) = 9300 points
• Max possible: 1700 votes × 6 = 10,200 points → 91% strength

LOW Strength (42%): Gets 200 first-choice, 300 fourth-choice, 800 sixth-choice
• Points: (200×6) + (300×3) + (800×1) = 1200 + 900 + 800 = 2900 points
• Max possible: 1300 votes × 6 = 7800 points → 37% strength

This shows: High strength = voters really wanted them; Low strength = voters ranked them reluctantly

Cross-Camp Appeal

▼
🧒 Explain Like I'm 5

Some candidates only appeal to people who think exactly like them. Others can win over people with different opinions. Cross-Camp Appeal measures how good a candidate is at bringing different types of voters together instead of just preaching to the choir.

Other ways to think about it: Bridge-Builder Score, Coalition Builder, How Well They Unite Different Groups

⚠️ Current Limitation: This metric currently shows 0% for all candidates because we don't yet have a way to categorize candidates into political groups (progressive, moderate, conservative, etc.). The algorithm is designed but needs candidate ideology data to work properly.

Low (20%)
Only appeals to similar voters Medium (50%)
Some crossover appeal High (80%+)
Unites diverse voters
🔢 How We Calculate It (Future Implementation)

Step 1: Categorize all candidates into ideological groups (progressive, moderate, conservative)

Step 2: For each voter who ranked this candidate, count how many different ideological groups their other choices represent

Step 3: Calculate average ideological diversity across all supporters

Formula: (Average Ideological Groups Ranked - 1) ÷ (Max Possible Groups - 1)

Data Needed: Manual candidate categorization or political endorsement analysis

📝 Examples (When Implemented)

High Cross-Camp (80%): Progressive candidate whose supporters also rank moderates and some conservatives - appeals across ideological lines

Low Cross-Camp (20%): Progressive candidate whose supporters only rank other progressives - stays within ideological bubble

This would show: Whether the candidate appeals across political divides or just within their own "camp"

Transfer Efficiency

▼
🧒 Explain Like I'm 5

In ranked-choice voting, if your favorite candidate can't win, your vote goes to your next choice. Transfer Efficiency measures how ready a candidate's supporters are to have their votes help someone else if needed.

Other ways to think about it: Backup Plan Score, How Well Supporters Prepare for Plan B, Vote Recycling Rate

Low (40%)
Few backup choices Medium (70%)
Some backup choices High (90%+)
Lots of backup choices
🔢 How We Calculate It

Step 1: Count voters who ranked this candidate

Step 2: Count how many also ranked other candidates after them

Step 3: Percentage with backup choices = Transfer Efficiency

Formula: (Voters with Next Choices) ÷ (Total Voters) = Transfer Efficiency

📝 Examples

HIGH Efficiency (85%): Candidate B has 1000 supporters
• 850 voters ranked other candidates after B (good backup plans)
• 150 voters only ranked B
• Result: 850 ÷ 1000 = 85% can transfer

LOW Efficiency (25%): Candidate C has 1000 supporters
• 250 voters ranked other candidates after C (few backup plans)
• 750 voters only ranked C (bullet voting)
• Result: 250 ÷ 1000 = 25% can transfer

This shows: High efficiency = votes stay in play; Low efficiency = votes get wasted when eliminated

Ranking Consistency

▼
🧒 Explain Like I'm 5

Some candidates get ranked in the same position by most of their supporters (very consistent). Others get scattered all over different rankings. Ranking Consistency measures how much voters agree about where this candidate belongs on their ballot.

Other ways to think about it: Voter Agreement Score, How Sure People Are About This Candidate, Clarity of Opinion

Low (20%)
Scattered rankings Medium (50%)
Some agreement High (80%+)
Strong agreement
🔢 How We Calculate It

Step 1: Look at which ranking positions this candidate received (1st, 2nd, 3rd, etc.)

Step 2: Calculate "entropy" - a measure of how scattered the data is

What is Entropy? Borrowed from physics/information theory - measures randomness:
• Low entropy = very organized (most votes in 1-2 positions)
• High entropy = very random (votes scattered across all positions)

Step 3: Convert entropy to consistency score

Formula:
• Entropy = -Σ(p × log₂(p)) for each ranking position percentage
• Consistency = 1 - (Entropy ÷ Maximum Possible Entropy)
• Result: 0% = scattered everywhere, 100% = all in one position

📝 Examples

HIGH Consistency (89%): 90% rank candidate #1, 10% rank #2
• Very low entropy - almost everyone agrees
• Clear consensus on candidate's position

LOW Consistency (15%): 20% rank #1, 18% rank #2, 17% rank #3, 15% rank #4, 16% rank #5, 14% rank #6
• Very high entropy - votes scattered everywhere
• No consensus on where candidate belongs

This shows: High consistency = voters agree about the candidate; Low consistency = voters very confused/divided

Polarization Index

▼
🧒 Explain Like I'm 5

Some candidates make people say "YES! I love them!" or "NO! I hate them!" Others make people say "Eh, they're okay." Polarization measures how much this candidate makes voters feel very strongly one way or another, instead of just being "meh."

Other ways to think about it: Controversy Score, How Much People Have Strong Feelings, Love-or-Hate Factor

Low (10%)
"Safe" candidate Medium (40%)
Some strong feelings High (70%+)
Very divisive
🔢 How We Calculate It

Step 1: Count "bullet voters" (people who ONLY ranked this candidate) - shows passionate exclusive support

Step 2: Count extreme ranking patterns - voters who rank them #1 or #6 (strong love/hate)

Step 3: Analyze ranking distribution spread - concentrated at extremes vs. middle positions

Step 4: Calculate ratio of extreme positions to moderate positions

Complete Formula:
• Base Score = (Bullet Voters ÷ Total Voters)
• Extreme Bonus = (Rank 1 + Rank 6 votes) ÷ (All Rankings) - 0.33 baseline
• Final Score = Base Score + max(0, Extreme Bonus)

Why it works: Both "only vote for them" and "rank them at extremes" indicate strong emotional responses

📝 Examples

HIGH Polarization (68%): Candidate causes strong reactions
• 15% bullet vote (rank ONLY them) - passionate fans
• 45% rank them #1, 8% rank them #6 - love/hate extremes
• Very few rank them #3, #4, #5 - no middle ground

LOW Polarization (8%): Candidate is broadly acceptable
• 2% bullet vote - few passionate exclusive supporters
• Rankings spread across #2, #3, #4 positions - seen as "fine"
• Rarely ranked #1 or #6 - not inspiring strong feelings

This shows: High polarization = "love or hate"; Low polarization = "safe consensus choice"

Transfer Pattern Type

▼
🧒 Explain Like I'm 5

When a candidate's votes transfer to others, do they mostly go to a few similar candidates (concentrated) or spread out to many different ones (dispersed)? This tells us how unified the candidate's supporters are in their backup choices.

Other ways to think about it: Backup Plan Unity, Where the Votes Go Next, Coalition Tightness

🔢 How We Calculate It

Concentrated: >50% of transfers go to top destination, or ≤3 major destinations

Dispersed: Transfers spread across many candidates relatively evenly

Mixed: Some concentration but also significant spreading

Analysis: Count transfer destinations and calculate distribution patterns based on vote counts, not ideology

Note: This is purely mathematical - we don't categorize candidates by political ideology

📝 Examples

CONCENTRATED Pattern: Candidate X's supporters have clear favorites
• 60% transfer to Candidate A, 25% to Candidate B
• Only 15% scattered among other candidates
• Shows unified coalition with shared preferences

DISPERSED Pattern: Candidate Y's supporters spread out widely
• Transfers go 15% each to 6 different candidates
• No candidate gets more than 18% of transfers
• Shows diverse coalition with different backup priorities

This shows: Concentrated = supporters think alike; Dispersed = supporters have very different politics

Remember: No single metric tells the whole story! Look at all of them together to understand how voters really felt about each candidate.
