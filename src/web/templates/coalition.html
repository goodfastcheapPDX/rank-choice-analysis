Deep dive into how votes actually flow between candidates using directional proximity analysis

Understanding Directional Analysis

This framework analyzes three fundamental questions about how voters connect two candidates:

üîÑ The 3 Core Questions:
  • Next Choice Rate (A ‚Üí B): Of ballots that ranked A anywhere, what % had B immediately after A?
  • Close-Together Rate (A & B): % of ballots that list both A and B within the top 3 spots (in any order)
  • Follow-Through (A ‚Üí B reality): When A was eliminated (or had surplus), what share actually landed on B?

Why This Matters: Directional analysis reveals asymmetric relationships. For example, A's supporters might consistently rank B next, but B's supporters might prefer other candidates after B.

What are Coalition Groups?

Coalition groups are candidates whose supporters tend to rank them similarly on their ballots. When voters consistently rank two candidates close together (like both in their top 3), it suggests these candidates appeal to similar voter bases or have aligned political positions.

üí° Interactive Tips:
  • Lower "Min Coalition Strength" (0.1-0.15): Find more groups, including loose alliances where candidates are sometimes ranked together
  • Higher "Min Coalition Strength" (0.3-0.4): Only very tight-knit candidate groups where voters consistently ranked them close together
  • Smaller "Min Group Size" (2-3): See pairs and small alliances
  • Larger "Min Group Size" (4-5): Only major political coalitions with many connected candidates

üìù Note: Groups are formed by connecting candidates who share enough ballots and have sufficient coalition strength, then finding all connected candidates in the network.

üîç How to Read the Results:
  • Groups with üèÜ: Contain winning candidates - successful coalitions
  • Group Size: Number of candidates who voters tend to rank together
  • Strength Score: How consistently voters rank these candidates close together (higher = stronger alliance)
  • Red candidate chips: Winners who formed effective coalitions

Understanding the Network

This network shows how candidates relate to each other based on real voter behavior. Each circle (node) is a candidate, and lines (edges) connect candidates whose supporters ranked them similarly.

üìä What the Sizes and Colors Mean:
  • Large circles: Candidates with high voter preference scores across all ranking positions

üéÆ How to Explore:
  • Click any candidate: Highlights their coalition network and dims others
  • Hover over candidates: See their weighted preference score and connection count
  • Hover over lines: See detailed coalition information between two candidates
  • Zoom in/out: Mouse wheel to focus on specific areas or see the big picture
  • Drag to pan: Move around the network to explore different regions
üìñ Key Terms:
  • "Shared Ballots": Ballots where both candidates appear (are ranked at any position). For example, if 150 voters ranked both Candidate A and Candidate B somewhere on their ballot, that's 150 shared ballots.
  • "Coalition Strength": How close together candidates were ranked on their shared ballots. Higher values mean voters consistently ranked them near each other (like both in top 3).
  • "Min Shared Ballots": Only show connections between candidates if at least this many voters ranked both of them.
üßÆ Example:

If you see a line between Candidate A and B with "Coalition Strength: 0.15" and your filter is set to "Min Shared Ballots: 100", this means:

  • At least 100 ballots ranked both A and B (anywhere on the ballot)
  • On those shared ballots, A and B were ranked close enough together to create a 0.15 strength score
  • For example: many voters put A as #2 and B as #3, or A as #1 and B as #4, etc.
üí° Try These Experiments:
  • Lower "Coalition Strength" to 0.15: See more connections, including looser alliances
  • Raise "Coalition Strength" to 0.4: Only the very strongest political partnerships
  • Lower "Min Shared Ballots" to 100: Include relationships from smaller voter groups
  • Raise "Min Shared Ballots" to 300: Only show relationships supported by very large numbers of voters
  • Filter by "Strong Only": Focus on the most significant political alliances
  • Click on a winner (red circle): See which other candidates formed coalitions with Portland's elected officials

üìä Why These Defaults? In a 20+ candidate race, appearing together on any ballot is meaningful. The default thresholds (200 shared ballots, 0.25 strength) are calibrated to show significant relationships while filtering out statistical noise.
